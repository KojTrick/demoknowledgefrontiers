Between Migration and Memory: Oral Traditions, Pulaaku, and the Intergenerational Transmission of Fulbe Cultural Knowledge in Southern Ghana

Carrying Culture Across Generations: Fulfulde Language, Oral Tradition, and Fulani Identity in Ghana


Abstract


This paper examines how Fulani (Fulbe) migrants in the Shai Osudoku District of Ghana's Greater Accra Region transmit, or struggle to transmit, cultural knowledge across generations, with particular attention to Fulfulde/Pulaar language competence and the oral tradition practices of storytelling, song, and ceremonial expression through which Fulbe identity and belonging are constituted. Drawing on interview data from fifteen participants across seven community sites, the paper finds that the communal evening storytelling ritual, through which Fulbe moral knowledge and cultural memory were historically conveyed, has collapsed within two generations, displaced by Qur'anic night-time education and digital technology. Simultaneously, Fulfulde is receding in ethnically mixed households, severing the linguistic channel through which oral tradition carries its culturally situated meaning. Transmission does not cease, however; contemporary Fulani music in Fulfulde is emerging as an informal carrier of language and Pulaaku values among youth, and participants across all sites consistently identified written and recorded documentation as the most viable safeguard against further loss. The paper argues that cultural preservation among Fulani migrants in Shai Osudoku requires attention not only to content but to the relational and communal contexts within which Fulbe cultural knowledge is learned, lived, and passed on, since it is the erosion of those contexts, rather than the absence of motivation, that most fundamentally drives the transmission failure this study documents.


Keywords: Fulani oral tradition, cultural transmission, Fulfulde language competence, cultural belonging, migration, Pulaaku identity, storytelling, cultural loss

 

Introduction



Migration into different cultural contexts carries significant consequences for the ways in which communities constitute, transmit, and sustain their cultural identities across generations (Osei-Tutu, 2024; 2022). For Fulani (Fulbe) migrants who settle in non-Fulbe host environments, these consequences are particularly acute, given that Fulbe cultural knowledge, encompassing the Fulfulde language, oral storytelling traditions, ceremonial songs, music, rituals, and the ethical code of Pulaaku, is historically transmitted through communal, relational, and contextually embedded practices that are profoundly sensitive to the structural and social disruptions that migration produces (Finnegan 2012; Vygotsky, 1978). When the evening storytelling circle dissolves, when ceremonial songs are no longer sung at weddings, when the living soundscape of Fulfulde recedes in households surrounded by other languages and cultures, what is lost is not merely content but the very infrastructure through which Fulbe cultural knowledge is learned, felt, and carried forward.

It is within this context that the present study examines how Fulani migrants living in the Shai Osudoku District of Ghana's Greater Accra Region transmit, or struggle to transmit, their cultural knowledge across generations, with particular attention to the role of language competence and oral tradition practices as interwoven sites of cultural survival, belonging, and intergenerational continuity. Specifically, the study investigated two main cultural domains: Fulfulde/Pulaar Language Competence, including transmission patterns, extent of use, generational decline, and emotional significance, and Rituals, Ceremonies, Music, and Storytelling, including frequency, adaptation, and transmission gaps.

The Fulani: A People in Motion

Long before colonial cartographers drew lines across the African continent, the Fulani were already moving. From the highlands of Futa Jalon in Guinea, they spread eastward across West Africa, from the Atlantic Coast to the Red Sea, carrying their cattle, their language, and their identity across every landscape they encountered (Setrana, 2021). Today they are found predominantly across Northern Nigeria, Ghana, Burkina Faso, Mali, Gambia, Mauritania, Benin, Ivory Coast, and Niger, with significant presence in Central Africa and the Sudan of East Africa. They call themselves Pullo in the singular, Fulbe in the plural, named differently by every neighbour who encountered them: Fulani by the Hausa, Peul by the Wolof, Fellata elsewhere, and Fulbe as an anglicisation of their own Fulfulde term increasingly adopted in academic and policy discourse. Through every name, they have remained one people, bound by their mother tongue, Fulfulde, and by a shared way of being in the world (Palmer and Johnston, 1923).

Their origins are themselves a story of many stories. Some trace them to Persia, others to India, and still others connect them to the Philistines of Biblical accounts. Certain scholars propose that the original Fulani crossed from Western Asia into Africa through Egypt long before the Christian era, while other views locate their ancestral roots among nomads from both North and sub-Saharan Africa who settled in the Senegal region and built the Tekrur Empire, a kingdom contemporaneous with the Ghana Empire, before dispersing across West Africa (Qadrimari, 2011; Idehen and Ikuru, 2019). What is agreed upon is this: wherever they went, they endured. Their world is the Sahel, that long, ancient belt of savannah and semi-arid land stretching across the continent, within which Fulani communities have lived for centuries (Mogtari, 2020; Oppong, 1999; Setrana, 2021; Fortes-Lima et al., 2025). Though widely known as West Africa's most pastoral ethnic group, the Fulani are not a monolithic people; large numbers are engaged in business, trade, manufacture, religious scholarship, and politics (Riesman, 1984; Bukari and Schareika, 2015), and their settlement patterns have shifted from traditional nomadism toward increasingly complex forms of sedentarism, with families residing in stable communities while rotating their herds toward water and pasture (Idehen and Ikuru, 2019).

Historical and Demographic Context on Fulani Migration into Ghana

Ghana is one chapter in this longer story of movement. Fulani migration into the country is a relatively recent phenomenon; it was not until the early twentieth century that any significant Fulani presence was documented in what was then the Gold Coast, with arrival in the northern regions around 1911 and numbers growing steadily to 300,000 by the 2000 Population and Housing Census (Tonah, 2005; Mogtari, 2020). Three forces drove this movement: the expansion of cattle trade, the establishment of colonial farming infrastructure, and the devastating Sahelian droughts of the 1970s and 1980s, which compelled herders to seek pasture and water beyond their homelands. Today, Fulani are present across urban Accra, peri-urban fringes, and rural communities, with particularly deep integration in the Bawku area and among the Ga Dangme groups of the Shai Osudoku area (Tonah, 2006; Oppong, 2017).

Yet settlement has never meant belonging, not in the eyes of the state and often not in the eyes of neighbours. Despite generations of residence, the Fulani remain legally classified as "aliens" and locally regarded as "strangers" (Oppong, 1999). Ghana's 1992 Constitution explicitly excludes from citizenship any person born in Ghana without a parent or grandparent also born there before 1957, a provision that places most Fulani outside the boundary of formal belonging regardless of how long their families have called Ghana home (Bukari and Schareika, 2015). Conflicts between Fulani herders and indigenous farmers over destroyed crops, land use, and scarce water have resulted in deaths, destruction of property, and cycles of mutual suspicion (Bukari and Schareika, 2015; Olayiwola et al., 2026). Multiple Ghanaian governments have responded with expulsion, a measure that has consistently failed. The Fulani, rendered invisible by law yet indispensable to the economies and ecologies they inhabit, have responded by negotiating belonging on their own terms, through community ties, through participation, through the quiet insistence of daily life (Isin, 2002; Soeters et al., 2017; Nartey and Ladegaard, 2021).


Pulaaku: The Fulani Way of Being

In the cultural life of the Fulani, identity is not merely inherited; it is lived, practised, and transmitted across generations through a deeply held philosophical code known as Pulaaku. Ezeomah (1997) observed that nomadic pastoral Fulani maintain a distinct cultural identity through strict adherence to this philosophical ideology. VerEecke (1991) defined the entire concept of Fulbe culture as "Pulaaku," structured around three cardinal virtues: Semteende, a sense of shame; hakkiilo, care and forethought; and munyal, endurance. Derrek Stenning described Pulaaku as the path guiding one toward justice, morality, and virtue, a quality many Fulani believe is peculiar to their own kind, a birth-acquired element found in the heart and blood of true Fulbe, whose purpose has always been both inward formation and outward distinction from other peoples (VerEecke, 1988; Qadrimari, 2011; Cobern et al., 1984).

Central to Pulaaku is Fulfulde, deeply descriptive and shaped by the lived experience of a nomadic people whose world demanded precise expression, evident in its four graduated degrees of the English word "small": pamara, pamarum, pamarel, pammetarel. Spoken across at least fifteen countries by over twelve million people, Fulfulde encompasses numerous dialects spanning Futa Toro, Futa Jallon, Masina, Burkina Faso, Sokoto, Gombe, and Adamawa (Pilaszewicz, 1996). Yet the language faces quiet erosion; Blench (1994) observed that urban Fulbe, across generations of settlement among other peoples, gradually shift toward the languages of those communities, with contact with Hausa and Arabic speakers and Islam's cultural influence tilting daily usage away from Fulfulde, such that even in Nigeria, where it has been officially targeted for promotion, the language remains in practical decline. Cattle herding is the third great pillar of Pulaaku, its significance extending far beyond economic life; herders move in attentive rhythm with the rain cycle, drawing near riverine areas in the dry season and retreating during the wet season to avoid the tsetse fly, their cattle grazing on grain stubble after harvest in a choreography of coexistence woven into the agricultural calendar (Cobern et al., 1984; Qadrimari, 2011; Stenning, 1959). It is within this layered context of movement, legal exclusion, and the daily practice of Pulaaku that the question at the centre of this study becomes most urgent: how are the Fulani migrants in Shai Osudoku surviving and belonging while transmitting Fulfulde language and oral tradition to the next generation?


Establishing the Gap

Scholarship on Fulani communities reveals mapped historical, demographic, and political circumstances across the Sahel and West Africa, yet how Fulani transmit cultural and linguistic knowledge across generations, especially in migrant contexts, remains underexamined. The literature emphasises conflict, identity, exclusion, and survival, but says little about everyday practices through which Fulani culture is learned, carried, or lost. At the global level, Fortes-Lima et al. (2025) trace Fulani dispersal across eleven Sahel countries, locating their linguistic and cultural homeland in West Africa. Awinia (2020), drawing on Tanzanian fieldwork and Blumer's symbolic interactionism, reframes pastoralist migration as socially constructed, mediated through language, ritual, and religion. Reviews by Sharifian et al. (2022) and Chao, Li, and Reyes-Garcia (2025) show pastoral knowledge as multifunctional yet threatened by social change, schooling, and abandonment of pastoralism. These syntheses highlight a gap: African pastoralist literature, especially on Fulani migrants, is thinly represented.

West African and Nigerian studies focus heavily on conflict. Sowale (2024) and others on farmer-herder violence detail exclusion, identity politics, and economic marginalisation shaping Fulani lives. Illiyasu and Abubakar (2025) study Fulfulde decline in Adamawa State, identifying English and Hausa dominance, weak policy support, and parental preferences as drivers of language shift. Their work, however, is geographically limited and methodologically anchored in survey perception data, leaving open how language and oral tradition operate together in Fulani families.

The Ghanaian literature, central to this study, is richer yet narrowly framed. Bukari and Schareika (2015) show Fulani pastoralists constructed as violent and non-citizens, leading to denial of land, settlement rights, and political inclusion. Nartey and Ladegaard (2021), analysing 160 news articles, identify discourses of dangerousness, alienisation, and stigmatisation, while noting their study did not engage Fulani voices directly. Three works move closer to cultural and identity dimensions: Setrana (2021) examines how 1.5 and second-generation Fulani negotiate citizenship and belonging through language and education, with Osudoku as one case site. Setrana, Kyei, and Nyarko (2022) document relations among autochthone farmers, sedentary Fulani, and nomadic Fulani in Agogo. Oppong's (2002) Moving Through and Passing On offers the most developed account of Fulani identity in Greater Accra, anchored in cattle herding, kinship, Pulaaku, Islam, and Fulfulde. Yet even these works treat language and oral tradition as background identity markers rather than central sites of investigation. This paper addresses this gap by examining how Fulani migrants in Shai Osudoku District transmit, or struggle to transmit, cultural knowledge across generations, focusing on Fulfulde and Pulaar competence and on rituals, ceremonies, music, and storytelling as lived and described by participants.


Theoretical Framework

This study draws on Cultural Transmission Theory (Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman, 1981; Hewlett and Cavalli-Sforza, 1986) as its primary structural scaffold, extended by frameworks more attentive to the relational, emotional, and culturally situated dimensions of transmission that the data foregrounds. Cultural Transmission Theory distinguishes three pathways through which cultural knowledge moves across generations: vertical transmission, from parent to child; oblique transmission, from community elders to younger non-relatives; and horizontal transmission, peer to peer. In traditional Fulbe society, transmission operated primarily through vertical and oblique channels, with children learning Pulaaku values, Fulfulde, oral storytelling, and ceremonial songs within a cohesive Fulbe social environment. What the data from Shai Osudoku reveals is not a uniform collapse of transmission but a highly differentiated one, in which oblique transmission has been weakened most severely, as families living among non-Fulbe populations have lost access to the community-based contexts within which those channels historically functioned. The theory's concept of transmission fidelity further helps to name what participants describe: not the total disappearance of cultural practice but its incomplete, diluted, or decontextualised reproduction in the next generation.

Yet Cultural Transmission Theory, rooted in an evolutionary and statistical model of how cultural traits spread across populations, was not designed to attend to the lived and emotionally weighted dimensions of transmission that an interpretive paradigm demands. It can map where the transmission chains have broken; it cannot account for the guilt carried by a young woman who did not choose the cultural gap she inhabits, or the quiet distress of a parent who does not know what to say when a child asks for a story. These are not peripheral dimensions of the phenomenon; they are constitutive of it. Vygotsky's (1978) sociocultural theory, and its elaboration in the context of language and cultural transmission (Lantolf and Thorne, 2006), provides the necessary complementary register by establishing that cultural knowledge is first constituted on the social level, in relational and contextually embedded interaction, before it is internalised by the individual. Finnegan (2012) deepens this understanding by establishing that African oral tradition is constituted through its conditions of performance and community gathering, such that when those conditions dissolve, what is lost is not simply the stories but the communal occasion within which stories made cultural knowledge emotionally legible to the young.

Together, the two frameworks allow this study to ask both the epidemiological question, which transmission routes have collapsed and why, and the experiential question, what it means to inhabit a cultural gap that one did not create. The first diagnoses the pathways of cultural erosion; the second explains the felt human cost of their collapse.


Methodology

This study employed a qualitative research design situated within an interpretive, community-centred paradigm, guided by Interpretive Phenomenological Analysis (IPA) as its primary analytical framework (Smith et al., 2009). IPA was selected for its capacity to illuminate how individuals make sense of their lived experiences, making it well suited to examining cultural transmission, identity, and intergenerational dynamics of Fulani language and oral tradition from within the communities most affected (Smith and Osborn, 2008; Larkin et al., 2006).

The Shai Osudoku District of Ghana's Greater Accra Region was selected as the study site for three intersecting reasons: its documented history of multi-generational Fulani settlement (Oppong, 2002; Setrana, 2021); its position as a non-Fulbe dominated peri-urban space where language contact pressures are particularly acute; and its absence from qualitative investigation of Fulani cultural transmission despite its prominence in demographic and conflict-focused scholarship (Bukari and Schareika, 2015; Nartey and Ladegaard, 2021). Together, these reasons position Shai Osudoku as a theoretically productive location at which intergenerational cultural transmission can be examined under conditions of legal exclusion, ethnic minority status, and structural precarity.

Participant selection followed a purposive sampling strategy (Patton, 2002), engaging fifteen Fulani community members across seven community sites, coded as AJ, DO, LO, MAN, OS, TO, and ZL. Participants were drawn from three generational cohorts, elders, parents, and youth, with individuals reporting both having and not having knowledge of oral traditions. The sample comprised nine female and six male participants. Interviews were conducted in English or through an interpreter where participants preferred Fulfulde, lasted approximately thirty to forty-five minutes, were audio-recorded with consent, and transcribed verbatim. Confidentiality was assured, with all identifying information replaced by alphanumeric codes.

Data analysis followed the six-stage IPA process (Smith et al., 2009), moving from close reading and annotation of individual transcripts through identification of experiential themes within each case to shared patterns of meaning across the dataset. This movement from the idiographic to the nomothetic honoured individual experience while building toward broader interpretive claims about how oral tradition knowledge moves, or ceases to move, between generations (Larkin et al., 2006). Trustworthiness was established through prolonged engagement with the data, reflexive journaling, and member checking where feasible (Lincoln and Guba, 1985).

Findings

The findings are organised around two cultural domains: Fulfulde/Pulaar language competence and the ritual, ceremonial, and storytelling practices through which Fulbe identity is constituted and transmitted. Across all seven community sites, these two domains emerged as mutually constitutive: the decline of oral tradition practices directly erodes the social contexts within which Fulfulde is learned and sustained, while the recession of Fulfulde removes the linguistic channel through which oral knowledge carries its culturally situated meaning.

Language as the Core Marker of Fulani Identity

Across all three generational cohorts, Fulfulde was positioned not merely as a medium of communication but as the living tissue through which Fulani identity, oral knowledge, and intergenerational belonging were constituted. A youth participant from the ZL site offered a compressed articulation: “even speaking your language makes you Fulor” (ZL1.F1, Youth). Yet this apparent consensus fractured under closer examination. An elder from the DO site grounded identity in genealogy rather than linguistic practice, arguing that “as for being a Fulani, even if you don’t understand the Fulani, you are still a Pulaar. If only you are born by a Pulaar” (DO2.M2, Elder). A parent from the LO site offered a more expansive account, locating Fulani identity in a constellation of embodied values: “the shyness. Yeah, you should be disciplined. Be a Muslim. Shyness and then staying in a bush to herd for cattle and then the language itself. Whatever your forefathers and your elders are doing, you should also adapt it. That will make you a Fulor” (LO2.M2, Parent). These positions differ in the degree to which language is seen as irreplaceable or substitutable within a broader assemblage of cultural markers, a tension that surfaces with particular urgency when read against the documented decline of Fulfulde across all sites.


Language Attrition Through Geographic Displacement

The most structurally significant finding within the language domain concerns the role of geographic location in accelerating language attrition. Participants living in areas with sparse Fulani populations were most acutely aware of this dynamic. A parent from the ZL site articulated the mechanism directly: “this place, we don’t have many Fulani in this area. So the language is making them lose their oral tradition because even some of the parents, if they keep long in the non-Fulani-dominated area, they learn the other language more than the Fulani” (ZL5.F4, Parent). By contrast, participants from the TO and LO sites, where Fulani community presence was relatively more concentrated, expressed greater confidence in domestic Fulfulde use. A parent from the TO site made explicit the co-dependency between language maintenance and oral tradition survival: “if I don’t speak Fulfulde with my children, they may lose their Fulani identity. Storytelling in Fulfulde helps strengthen the language” (TO5.F3, Parent). This formulation positions oral tradition not as dependent on language but as necessary to language’s own social vitality.


Language, Music, and Adaptive Cultural Transmission

Contemporary Fulani music emerged as the most robust surviving channel through which Fulfulde was being maintained among younger generations. A parent from the MAN site offered the clearest account: “nowadays, Fulani music has been modernised. Artists have put our language into modern music styles. Children enjoy those songs. For example, there is an artist called Aisha Ghana, her songs are in Fulfulde, and the children love them. Even though it is not like the old songs, at least it keeps the language alive” (MAN5.M2, Parent). Youth participants across the AJ and ZL sites confirmed this pattern, with one noting: “I know songs by Aisha Ghana and Biliadi, who are Fulani musicians. I learn basic things about how to care for yourself and others. The songs also talk about Pulaaku, the Fulani code of conduct, and teach us about good behaviour” (AJ3.F2, Youth). However, a parent from the TO site introduced a qualification: her child could sing along to Fulani music but was “still too young to know which songs are Fulani and which are not” (TO5.F3, Parent). This ambivalence between music as cultural carrier and music as background exposure represents an important internal tension within the adaptive transmission dynamic.


Emotional Significance of Language Loss

The emotional register in which participants spoke about language was most clearly articulated in relation to identity and communal dignity rather than functional competence. A youth from the AJ site resisted the expectation of regret about not knowing oral tradition, stating plainly that she still felt connected as a Fulani without it (AJ4.F3, Youth). A parent from the TO site, however, situated the emotional stakes in sharper terms: “Fulani people should always be proud of the Fulbe identity. Even if someone is educated, they should not respond in English when their fellow Fulani greets them in Fulfulde” (TO5.F3, Parent). The emotional experience of not knowing stories and proverbs, distinct from language competence itself, is addressed further in the oral tradition findings below.


Rituals, Ceremonies, Music, and Storytelling
The Communal Evening Storytelling Ritual

The most structurally significant finding across this domain is the near-total collapse of the communal evening storytelling ritual within one to two generations. Participants who possessed oral tradition knowledge described, with remarkable consistency across sites, a highly structured social occasion: after supper, children gathered around fire or lamp, an elder presided, and the session opened through call and response before the stories began. A parent from the LO site recalled: “they normally tell us at night after supper. They gather us and then set fire. Then we sit by it and they tell us the story” (LO2.M2, Parent). An elder from the DO site described the same ritual with its performative conventions intact: “they will say tale tale, then you say talata, then you respond talata” (DO2.M2, Elder), before explaining the parable’s meaning through the image of three hearth stones that together hold a cooking pot. A parent from the MAN site situated the setting within its broader material conditions: “in those days, most people didn’t have electricity or television. So on days when we didn’t have that, we all sat together, sometimes with our mothers, grandmothers, or grandfathers, around a lamp. We would listen quietly. If someone interrupted, they’d be told to keep quiet because it was something serious” (MAN5.M2, Parent). What these accounts collectively establish is that the storytelling ritual was not simply an informal activity but a disciplined social institution with its own protocols, hierarchy, and emotional register.

Ceremonial Traditions in Transition

The most visible dimension of ceremonial decline concerns the transformation of wedding and communal celebration practices. An elder from the DO site described this shift: “those days during my parents’ time, when it comes to dancing and singing, they used calabash to make their ceremonies. But these days we don’t see that. Whenever there is a marriage, they have to go hire JAMS and the jams they play a whole lot of songs: Christian songs, Fulbe songs, Western songs all over. So, the culture has changed” (DO2.M2, Elder). A parent from the MAN site further observed that drums and songs once served a mnemonic function: “we used drums and songs to tell stories. That rhythm helped me remember them easily” (MAN1.F1, Parent). The replacement of participatory, community-produced ceremonial music with commercially rented entertainment signals the dissolution of the social occasion within which oral tradition was performed and transmitted.

The data also surfaces a clear generational divergence in proverb use. Participants from the DO and LO sites still use proverbs in everyday speech and report that children understand them with explanation (DO2.M2, Elder; LO2.M2, Parent). A parent from the MAN site uses them but notes that children rarely grasp the meaning without elaboration, citing the example: “if you see your brother’s beard burning, soak your own in water” (MAN5.M2, Parent). A parent from the TO site considers proverbs outmoded and does not use them at all (TO5.F3, Parent). This divergence suggests that proverb transmission is not uniformly lost but is differentially maintained depending on the density of the Fulbe social environment and individual parental practice.


Transmission Gaps Across Generations and Sites

The transmission gaps produced by the collapse of storytelling ritual and ceremonial practice are experienced differently across generations and sites. Youth described the absence primarily as personal loss: “now those storytelling does not even exist. I would have loved to teach my children though, but it doesn’t even exist anymore” (LO3.M3, Youth). An elder from MAN site located the gap in the indifference of the audience: “my children are not interested. They tell me it is outmoded” (MAN1.F1, Elder). A parent from TO site gave the structural summary: “most families have stopped. People are busy. Children are on phones” (TO5.F3, Parent).

Accompanying this structural failure is an emotional dimension that cuts across sites and generations. Youth who lack storytelling knowledge describe guilt about a gap they did not choose. One stated: “I feel guilty, I don’t know stories, but I still want to learn when I get the chance” (ZL1.F1, Youth), while another framed the same regret through the research encounter itself: “I wish I have listened to those stories now because right now you are here to ask me questions on story. If I have knowledge on stories, by now I would have given you much details” (LO3.M3, Youth). Not all participants share this; a youth from the AJ site expressed no regret, seeing storytelling as outmoded (AJ4.F3, Youth). The divergence between guilt and indifference across the youth cohort reflects the uneven pace at which the loss is being normalised across different sites and household contexts.

Written and Recorded Preservation as a Community Response

Across all sites and generational cohorts, participants consistently identified written and recorded preservation as the most viable response to the collapse of oral transmission. An elder from the AJ site stated: “I think they should be written down in books” (AJ2.F1, Elder). A parent from the ZL site grounded this view in the risk of elder mortality: “It should be written down because now we don’t know who is going to die and leave who. When they write it, any generation that comes will come and get the chance to read and know” (ZL5.F4, Parent). A youth from the LO site proposed a concrete process: “We have people who have a lot of kno.wledge about the Fulbe tradition so we can just go to them, sit beside them and play our recording. They tell us all the narratives then we write it down and keep it for future use” (LO3.M3, Youth). A parent from the MAN site extended this to digital platforms, suggesting that stories and lessons be put on social media so children would encounter and learn them (MAN5.M2, Parent). This pattern is one of the most consistent findings across the dataset, cutting across age, site, and knowledge status, and it stands as the community’s own articulated response to transmission failure.


Discussion

What the data reveal from Shai Osudoku is not simply a record of cultural loss, though loss is present, but a more layered account of what it costs to carry a living culture across geographic and generational distance without the relational infrastructure that once made that carrying possible. The erosion of Fulfulde and Pulaar language competence and the collapse of oral traditional practices are not parallel or separable processes; they are mutually constitutive, each accelerating the other, and each drawing its meaning from the same foundational question of whether Fulbe identity can survive the conditions of its own displacement.

The Shai Osudoku data show the collapse of oblique transmission of the Cultural Transmission Theory’s tripartite framework (Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman, 1981; Hewlett and Cavalli-Sforza, 1986) which is particularly consequential for cultural continuity because it distributes the burden of knowledge reproduction across multiple adults, thereby buffering against the loss of any single transmitter (Cavalli-Sforza & Feldman, 1981). The Shai Osudoku data reveal precisely the inverse: migration into a non-Fulbe dominated district has severed the lateral connections among community members, leaving the transmission burden concentrated in individual households where parents themselves often lack the knowledge they would need to pass on. Vygotsky’s (1978) account of the zone of proximal development deepens this analysis. If cultural learning occurs first in collaboration with a more capable other, the collapse of oblique transmission is not simply a reduction in the quantity of knowledge available; it removes the mediating relationship through which cultural knowledge becomes internalised. The elder was not merely a content provider; he or she was the scaffolding through which Pulaaku values, Fulfulde idioms, and the moral logic of the stories became emotionally legible to the young. Lantolf et al. (2020) offer a sustained elaboration of this scaffolding dynamic in language and cultural learning contexts. Wertsch (1991) extends Vygotsky’s concept through the notion of mediated action, arguing that when the cultural tool and its social context of use are separated, the knowledge it carried cannot be reconstituted simply by reintroducing the tool in isolation.

The sub-theme of language attrition is consistent with, and extends, the patterns documented by Illiyasu and Abubakar (2025) in Adamawa State, where English and Hausa dominance, weak policy support, and parental accommodation of majority-language environments drive language shift among Fulani communities. This study confirms this pattern in the Ghanaian context, where English and local Ghanaian languages (Dangbe) exert comparable structural pressure on everyday Fulfulde use. Setrana (2021) corroborates this dynamic by documenting how second-generation Fulani in Greater Accra negotiate language and identity under conditions of educational and legal marginalisation. However, the emotional and identity weight that Fulfulde carries across all three generations, and the role of contemporary music as a partial compensatory mechanism is where this study extends on the pattern. ZL5.F4 captured the mechanism with precision: parents who remain for extended periods in non-Fulbe dominated areas gradually shift toward surrounding languages, such that children grow up with limited Fulfulde competence even within Fulbe households. Darvin and Norton’s (2015) investment model illuminates the structural position of Fulfulde in Shai Osudoku: participants across all cohorts affirm the language’s identity value, yet outside the Fulbe community, Fulfulde carries limited social capital, shaping the family language decisions that Fishman (1991) and Spolsky (2004) identify as central to language vitality. Grenoble and Whaley (2006) establish that endangered minority languages require not only affective loyalty but also the institutional and community infrastructure that makes daily use possible. The Shai Osudoku communities have the loyalty; what they lack is the infrastructure.

The displacement of evening storytelling by Qur’anic education is analytically distinct from technology-driven displacement and the existing literature has not fully accounted for it. Elder participants do not experience this as a conflict between religion and culture, since the Quran is understood as integral to Fulbe identity. The displacement is temporal and structural: night-time, once the natural occasion for oral tradition in community life, has been reallocated to religious education through community-wide decisions. This finding extends Illiyasu and Abubakar’s (2025) identification of religious education as a driver of Fulfulde decline by specifying the mechanism: it is not the content of Islamic education that competes with oral tradition but the temporal structure it imposes, which removes the one daily window within which the social conditions for storytelling were historically met. Adeyemi and Adeyinka (2003) situate this tension within the broader contest between indigenous African educational traditions and imported formal and religious educational structures, a framework that positions the Shai Osudoku case within a continent-wide pattern rather than a community-specific anomaly. From a Vygotskian (1978) perspective, the call-and-response structure of the storytelling ritual, the fire or lamp, the elder’s presence, and the communal gathering constituted the zone of proximal development through which Fulbe cultural knowledge was transmitted in ways that neither written records nor informal daytime conversation can replicate. Thus, understanding of learning as fundamentally situated within mediated social contexts illuminates why the removal of the storytelling context does not simply reduce the quantity of transmission but dissolves the conditions within which transmission was organic and affectively meaningful. Coupled with the removal of storytelling is the transformation of cultural ceremonies like funerals and weddings where traditional music and oral storytelling used to be a paramount feature. However, these have given way to more contemporary modes such as using DJs, a phenomenon that reflects loss but noting the earlier impact of music on Fulbe language development, produces a nuances in cultural transmission for the Fulbe/Fulani in the study area.

The emergence of contemporary Fulani music as an informal carrier of Fulfulde and Pulaaku values represents the study’s most analytically productive sub-theme. Within Cultural Transmission Theory, this pattern maps onto horizontal transmission, knowledge shared between peers in the same generation, carried here through digitally mediated musical consumption. Finnegan (2012) established that African oral tradition is a living, adaptive system that renegotiates its forms as the conditions of performance change, and the migration of Fulbe cultural values into contemporary music genres is consistent with this adaptive logic. Grondin (2021), Gwerevende and Mthombeni (2023), and Tibi (2025) each provide comparative evidence from other minority and heritage language contexts where contemporary music functions as an adaptive transmission channel in response to the collapse of direct pedagogy. What is analytically significant is the partial nature of what music carries: songs sustain the language and transmit elements of the Pulaaku moral code, as youth participants across sites confirm, but they do not carry the pedagogical depth of storytelling, the communal occasion of ceremony, or the interactional complexity through which proverbs teach. The adaptation is real but incomplete. This pattern, in which emotional and identity attachment persists even as functional competence declines, and language transmission through music is consistent with Darvin and Norton's (2015) investment model, which locates language identity at the intersection of ideology, capital, and social power. In Shai Osudoku, Fulfulde carries deep emotional value within the community but limited social capital beyond it, and this structural asymmetry shapes the family language decisions that Fishman (1991) and Spolsky (2004) identify as central to language vitality.

The emotional register of the findings, characterised by guilt among youth who did not choose the cultural gap they inhabit, quiet regret among parents who lack the knowledge or time to transmit what they value, and collective appeals to pride among elders, cannot be adequately accounted for by Cultural Transmission Theory alone. The theory maps pathways; it does not account for the weight that inhabiting a broken pathway carries. What the data shows is that the emotional experience of transmission failure is primarily guilt about an inherited gap and pride in an identity that participants refuse to relinquish, rather than political dispossession or indifference. The framework offered here, combining Cultural Transmission Theory with Vygotsky’s sociocultural theory, accounts for both the structural and the phenomenological dimensions of this experience: where the former identifies the broken pathways, the latter explains why those pathways cannot simply be rebuilt by individual will, because the knowledge they carried was constituted in a social form that the conditions of migration in Shai Osudoku have not allowed to persist.

It is worth acknowledging the limits of both theoretical frameworks in the light of these findings. Cultural Transmission Theory was developed within an evolutionary and statistical tradition and was not designed to attend to the historically specific and emotionally weighted character of cultural loss as lived by a particular community. Vygotsky’s sociocultural theory, while attentive to the relational character of learning, does not without extension address the dynamics of minority cultural knowledge under conditions of structural exclusion. The theoretical architecture this study proposes is explicitly a productive combination: Cultural Transmission Theory provides the structural scaffold, and sociocultural theory provides the experiential depth, but neither alone is sufficient for understanding what it costs to carry a living culture across geographic and generational distance without the relational infrastructure that once made that carrying possible. Future research might extend this framework by incorporating Bourdieu’s (1986) concept of cultural capital, which would allow the social and symbolic stakes of Fulbe cultural knowledge to be more precisely theorised in relation to the field of power within which Shai Osudoku Fulani communities seek recognition. Swidler (1986) offers a complementary resource through the concept of culture as a toolkit, which would allow the study to account for the selective, strategic ways in which participants retain some elements of Fulbe culture, such as language loyalty and contemporary music, while releasing others, such as proverbs and ceremonial songs.

The picture that emerges is not one of total loss. What the data reveal is a pattern of fragmented continuity, in which specific elements of Fulbe cultural knowledge persist in adapted or displaced forms while others have effectively ceased to be transmitted. Connerton (1989) establishes that cultural continuity depends not on the preservation of content alone but on the preservation of the embodied, habitual, and communal practices through which content is carried; the loss of the storytelling occasion is, in his terms, a loss of the commemorative ceremony through which knowledge becomes social memory. Songs preserve language and emotional connection but do not carry the pedagogical function of storytelling. Proverbs survive in occasional parental usage but without the systematic pedagogy that once ensured they were understood. The Fulbe identity persists in emotional attachment and linguistic loyalty even where functional competence has declined. Participants know what they value; what they lack are not incentives but contexts.


Implications

The implications of these findings are grounded in the recognition that cultural preservation cannot be addressed as a content problem alone, as a question of which stories should be written down or which songs should be recorded, without simultaneously attending to the relational conditions within which Fulbe cultural knowledge has historically been reproduced. Migration has shifted transmission from communal to individual channels, compressing what was once a distributed, multigenerational, and publicly performed practice into the private and often overburdened space of the household. Distinguishing between total loss and fragmented continuity matters here: fragmented continuity signals that the motivational and affective resources for preservation remain present but are unattached to the structural conditions that would make preservation sustainable. Participants know what they value. What they lack are not incentives but contexts.

At the community level, the findings point to the need for a community-based oblique transmission program modelled on language nest initiatives documented in indigenous revitalisation contexts (Grenoble and Whaley, 2006). This would create structured spaces in which elders with oral tradition knowledge can transmit stories, proverbs, and Fulfulde to younger generations in a form that approximates the social occasion within which that transmission was historically organic. This recommendation is grounded in participants' expressed desire to learn and their regret about knowledge gaps, rather than an explicit call for structured programs.

The role of contemporary Fulani music as an adaptive transmission channel points to the value of supporting Fulbe artists who carry the language and values of Pulaaku into digitally accessible forms. Artists such as Aisha Ghana and Biliadi, named by participants across multiple sites, are already performing an informal preservation function. Institutional and financial support for such artists would build on a transmission mechanism that is already active, youth-facing, and confirmed by the data as the most robust surviving channel through which Fulfulde and Pulaaku values currently reach the younger generation.

The community's own call for written and recorded documentation represents a third clear implication. Across sites, generations, and knowledge profiles, participants identified this as the most realistic and accessible means of safeguarding what remains of Fulani oral tradition, particularly given the risk of elder mortality and geographic dispersal. Investment in community-based documentation projects, in which elders with knowledge are recorded systematically and materials produced in Fulfulde for community access, responds directly to what participants said. This does not substitute for relational transmission but represents an intentional attempt by the community to safeguard their cultural ethos.

The paper's central argument emerges from this convergence. Perceived cultural loss among Fulani migrants in Shai Osudoku is fundamentally entangled with the struggle for belonging and recognition in a new environment, in ways that neither the conflict-focused nor the governance-focused strands of the Ghanaian literature have previously examined. Setrana (2021) has shown that second-generation Fulani herders construct belonging through lived practice rather than legal status. Bukari and Schareika (2015) have shown that public and policy discourse consistently denies them the recognition belonging requires. Oppong (2002) has argued that Fulani identity in Greater Accra is anchored in the expressive and linguistic forms through which belonging is performed. None of these studies, however, focused on the cultural practices through which the Fulbe define and maintain their identity over time. This study fills that gap by showing that those very practices are the ones most at risk when the Fulbe are displaced from their land or shut out from society. Preservation efforts that attend only to content will remain incomplete unless they also attend to the relational and linguistic contexts within which Fulbe cultural knowledge is learned, lived, and passed on. It is in the recovery of those contexts, as much as in the recovery of the knowledge they carry, that the future of Fulbe cultural continuity in Shai Osudoku will be decided. Conclusion

This study set out to examine how Fulani migrants in Shai Osudoku transmit, or struggle to transmit, Fulfulde language competence and oral tradition knowledge across generations, and what it finds is not a story of total loss but of fragmented continuity, in which emotional attachment to Fulbe identity and adaptive cultural expression through contemporary music persist even as the communal and relational infrastructure through which Fulfulde was learned, stories were told, and Pulaaku was performed has been systematically dismantled by migration, religious reallocation, and the pressures of a non-Fulbe dominated environment. By combining Cultural Transmission Theory with Vygotsky's sociocultural theory, the study demonstrates that the collapse of oblique transmission is not simply a reduction in the content available to younger generations but a dissolution of the mediated social contexts within which that content was made meaningful, and that rebuilding cultural continuity therefore requires the recovery of contexts, not only the recovery of knowledge. In doing so, the study fills a gap that the conflict-focused and governance-focused strands of the Ghanaian Fulani literature have left open, showing that the cultural practices through which the Fulbe define and sustain their identity over time are precisely the practices most at risk when communities are displaced from their land and shut out from the societies in which they settle, and that the future of Fulbe cultural continuity in Shai Osudoku will be decided not only by what is written down or recorded but by whether the conditions that once made cultural transmission organic, the elder, the gathering, the shared evening, and the intergenerational recognition that what is being passed on matters, can be recovered in some form within the communities that need them most.
